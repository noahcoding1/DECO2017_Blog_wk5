## Choosing Our Idea - Group Discussion, Research and Early Planning

After the first stage of idea generation, the next step was to compare our ideas as a group and decide which community had the strongest potential for the BlaBla Corp community hub. Our main focus was to consider not only what seemed interesting, but also what could realistically support repeat interaction, user-generated content, and a clear prototype direction.

To keep the discussion focused, we tried out a stand-up style meeting over Zoom, where each member shared what they had considered, what ideas they thought had potential, and any concerns about feasibility. This helped move the conversation away from simply choosing the most interesting topic and towards thinking about which community would best fit the project brief. We also set up a simple Kanban board in our Google Docs with “To do”, “In progress” and “Done” columns. Although this was a basic project management tool, it helped us make the work more visible and begin separating research, ideation, wireframing and later technical planning.

![Group Kanban board](assets/images/Kanban.png)

*Figure 1: Our group Kanban board showing “To do”, “In progress” and “Done” columns.*

The main design decision this week was choosing home cooks as our community. We each considered other ideas, but home cooks stood out because the community naturally creates both structured information and social interaction. Recipes can be organised by cuisine, ingredients, dietary needs and difficulty, while the social side comes from users sharing outcomes, asking questions, giving feedback and suggesting changes. This made the idea feel more suitable than a static recommendation site or a broad interest page.

However, there was also an important segment in our meeting where we discussed how a home cooking hub could easily become too generic because recipe platforms already exist. If our idea was only “users can post recipes”, it would not be meaningfully different from existing websites. This made us think about what home cooks actually need from online communities, rather than assuming that recipe sharing alone was enough. To answer this, we needed to do more research.

![Group decision table comparing community ideas](assets/images/Decision_Plan.png)

*Figure 2: Group decision with risks and prototype potential.*

To explore this, I conducted online ethnography while Sanjana conducted competitor analysis. I did this by observing how home cooks interact on existing platforms such as recipe websites, Reddit-style cooking forums, TikTok cooking comments, Pinterest recipe boards, and other food communities. A clear pattern was that people were not only looking for finished recipes. They were often asking practical questions: how to substitute ingredients, how to fix mistakes, how to adapt recipes for dietary needs, or whether a method would still work with different equipment. This suggested that home cooking is often collaborative and problem-solving based.

![Online ethnography notes](assets/images/Online_Ethnography.png)

*Figure 3: Online ethnography notes showing observed behaviours such as substitution questions, feedback, recipe fixes and adaptations.*

This research shifted our thinking. Instead of designing a platform mainly around displaying recipes, we started considering how the hub could support adaptation and discussion around recipes. This was a more specific direction because it responded to an observed behaviour: home cooks often modify recipes rather than following them exactly. The design opportunity became less about storing perfect recipes and more about helping users learn from each other’s changes, mistakes and improvements, while also allowing people to form communities and have discussions.

![Ideas breakdown from research insights](assets/images/Features)

*Figure 4: Ideas breakdown showing how research insights led to possible features such as recipe adaptations, discussion spaces, saved recipes and user groups.*

Overall, this week helped us move from a broad community choice to a more justified design direction. The most important outcome was recognising the main risk in our concept, as we have to make sure it does not become just another recipe-sharing platform and instead has a spark that makes it unique. Early online ethnography helped address this risk by showing that home cooks need spaces for adaptation, troubleshooting and feedback.
